Lola's First "Text"
"Mama, check your doorstep!"
When Maria moved abroad for work, her 78-year-old grandmother, Lola Rosa, refused to learn smartphones.
Letters became their lifeline—but postal delays made Maria miss birthdays and holidays. With Courier,
Maria now drafts letters on her phone during lunch breaks. Lola Rosa receives them in handwritten-style
print within days, complete with Maria’s scanned doodles of their old cat. One morning, Lola Rosa opened
her door to find a Courier envelope with a single line: "I love you"—typed by Maria but delivered like a
hug. That night, Lola left a voicemail: "Your ‘text’ arrived. Write me again tomorrow."

The Soldier's Secret Promise
Deployed in a remote base with spotty internet, Jake relied on Courier to send
encrypted letters to his wife, Emma. The app’s military-grade security ensured
his messages about their unborn child stayed private. One letter, marked
"Open when the baby kicks," arrived as Emma felt the first flutter.
Inside was a folded ultrasound image Jake had uploaded digitally,
now printed alongside his vow: "I’ll read to them every night—even
through these letters." When Jake finally held his daughter, she
recognized his voice from the Courier letters Emma had read aloud.

The Grief That Found Its Way
After losing his brother, David couldn’t bring himself to delete their old texts.
His therapist suggested writing letters to process his guilt over unsaid words.
But David’s hands shook too much to hold a pen. With Courier, he typed raw,
rambling notes—auto-formatted into elegant stationery—and sent them to his
own address. One day, he accidentally addressed a letter to his brother’s
old home. The new tenant, an elderly man, replied via Courier:
"Your words found me. I lost a sister too. Keep writing."
Their unexpected pen-pal friendship became David’s
bridge back to healing.









Sender: David, 41
